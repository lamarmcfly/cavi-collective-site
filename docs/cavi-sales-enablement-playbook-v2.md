# Part 1. Know Cavi

---

## 1.1 The One-Sentence Pitch

> **"Cavi gives operators coordinated AI teams that give humans superpowers."**

Note: It says **superpowers** — capability expansion. Not "AI replaces your team."

**Secondary value statement** (for cost-driven conversations):

> "Coordinated AI specialists working under your team's direction — building capacity, freeing budget, and creating workflows that compound over time."

Both are approved. Lead with the primary in headlines, decks, and first impressions. Lead with the secondary when the prospect opens with cost concerns, budget constraints, or headcount pressure.

---

## 1.2 The 30-Second Story

> Most teams are stuck: hire slowly and grow slowly, or hire fast and burn cash. Cavi breaks that trade-off. We give operators a coordinated AI workforce — **66+ specialists organized across 8 coordinated divisions — and growing** — that plugs into their business in three days, runs under their direction, and scales output without scaling headcount. Your team approves, the AI executes. You get the leverage of a 50-person org with the focus of a 10-person team.

**Use this** when someone says "So what does Cavi do?" at a conference, on a call, or in a cold email opener. Thirty seconds. Pause. Let them ask the next question.

---

## 1.3 The Platform in Plain English

Cavi is the **Agentic Operations Platform — AOP.** Think of it as a managed AI workforce, not a tool.

What that means concretely:

- **66+ AI specialists — and growing.** Each specialist has a narrow job, a defined scope, and a name. They are not chatbots. They are not general-purpose assistants. Each one does one thing well, coordinates with the others, and delivers reviewable work. The roster expands continuously as we develop new capabilities.

- **8 coordinated divisions.** Executive Operations, Engineering & Development, Marketing & Brand, AI Media Production, Talent Acquisition, Project Operations & Analytics, Research & Intelligence, and Infrastructure & Reliability. Specialists inside a division coordinate with each other automatically. Divisions coordinate across boundaries when work requires it. Additional divisions are in development.

- **Labor as a Service (LAaS).** Clients pay a monthly subscription per specialist or per department. Each subscription includes a **monthly Model Budget** that covers typical operating volume. If a client exceeds it, additional model usage is passed through at our wholesale rate with **zero markup**. No per-seat math, no surprises, full transparency.

- **Human in the loop at every gate.** Your team reviews, approves, and directs. Cavi never ships work that a human did not green-light. Every output passes through an approval gate before it reaches production. The human is always in command.

- **Live in 3 days.** Not weeks. Not a six-month implementation. Three days from pilot kickoff to first specialist deployed and producing reviewable output.

> **What "Live in 3 days" actually means**
>
> The 3-day promise refers to your **first specialist deployed and producing reviewable output**. That means: onboarding call on Day 1, configuration and integration on Day 2, first live output reviewed by your team on Day 3. Complex multi-division deployments set realistic timelines during scoping — but even those start producing value within the first week. The point is speed: you see real work before you have time to second-guess the decision.

>

## 1.5 Pre-Launch Is an Asset, Not a Liability

When a prospect asks "How many customers do you have?" — and they will — do not flinch. Lean in.

> "We're pre-launch and that's deliberate. We're hand-selecting a small group of founding customers who get **founder-level attention**, **founding pricing protections**, and **a real seat at the table on what we build next**. Early customers shape the product. Later customers use the product. We're looking for people who want to shape it."

**Why this works:**

- It reframes scarcity as exclusivity.
- It signals that the founders are in the room, not delegating to a support team.
- It creates urgency: the founding window closes.
- It attracts operators who want influence, not just software.

**If they push back:** "Fair question. We've been running the platform internally for months. The output projections come from real work, not demos. The pilot exists specifically so you can see it working on your workflows before you commit."

If they lead with capacity pressure, lead with superpowers. Both are true.

# Part 2. Know the Buyer — THREE SEGMENTS, EQUAL PRIORITY

---

> **IMPORTANT: Cavi sells into three segments with EQUAL priority.** Each has a different buyer, a different pain pattern, and a different close. Learn all three. Do not blend them. Do not skip the solopreneur because the check is smaller. Do not skip the enterprise because the cycle is longer. Every segment matters. Every deal matters.

---

## 2.1 Three Segments, Equal Priority

| Segment | Revenue Range | Decision Maker | Why This Segment Matters |
|---------|--------------|----------------|------------------------|
| **Solopreneur / 1-person ops** | $0-$2M | The founder (they ARE the company) | Highest emotional fit. They feel the pain of being alone. Solo tier at $1,200/mo is a no-brainer if discovery lands. Fastest close, highest satisfaction, strongest word-of-mouth. |
| **SMB (20-500 employees)** | $2M-$50M | Founder, COO, VP Marketing, VP Ops | Fastest decisions, real budgets, founders still in the room. Our pricing fits without a calculator. Our 3-day deploy story matches their urgency. Bread and butter. |
| **Enterprise ($10M+)** | $10M-$500M+ | CTO, VP Ops, VP Innovation, Chief AI Officer | Bigger checks, deeper relationships, expansion potential. Pursue when the entry point is a real operator, not a procurement lead with a 200-question RFP and no authority. |

**The rule:** No segment is "primary." No segment is "secondary." They are different plays in the same playbook. Run whichever play the field gives you.

---

## 2.2 Solopreneur Persona — "The Founder of One"

**Who they are:**

- Solo founders, indie operators, consultants billing $150-$500/hr
- Building a SaaS, agency, content business, or productized service — alone
- Already paying for 5-15 SaaS tools and comfortable with subscription spend in the $200-$2,000/month range
- Identifies as "resourceful" — proud of doing more with less
- Active on Twitter/X, LinkedIn, indie hacker communities, or niche Slack groups
- Has tried VA services, freelancers, and every AI tool on Product Hunt

**What keeps them up at night:**

- "Every hour I spend on ops is an hour I am not spending on revenue."
- "I cannot afford a full hire and I am tired of stitching tools together."
- "I want leverage, not more software."
- "I am one illness away from my business stopping."
- "I know AI can help me but I do not have time to figure out how."

**Their decision process:**

They decide alone. Fast. Usually within one conversation. The question is not "Can I afford this?" — it is "Does this feel like it will actually work, or is it another shiny thing that takes more time than it saves?" They are skeptical because they have been burned by tools that promised automation and delivered configuration.

**How to sell to solopreneurs:**

The Solo tier at **$1,200/month** is the entire pitch. Frame it as "a specialist who works 24/7, costs less than one freelance project per month, and lives inside your business in three days." Solopreneurs need a **10-minute demo and a clear next step**. Move fast. Do not over-explain the platform architecture. Do not talk about divisions. Talk about the one specialist that will change their Tuesday.

**Approved opening line for solopreneurs:**

> "What if you had a specialist that handled [their specific pain] 24/7, and it cost less than a single freelancer invoice?"

---

## 2.3 SMB Primary Persona — "The Stretched Operator"

**Who they are:**

- Founder, COO, VP Marketing, or VP Operations at a 20-500 person company
- Revenue between $2M and $50M
- Has tried hiring agencies, freelancers, offshore teams — at least one engagement failed
- Already uses ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini personally but does not have an AI "team"
- Runs on a mix of Slack, Notion, HubSpot, Jira, and duct tape
- Their team is good but undersized for the workload

**What keeps them up at night:**

- "I have more demand than capacity to deliver against."
- "Hiring takes 90 days and costs me $80K loaded — and the person might not work out."
- "My team is burning out and I can see it."
- "I keep seeing AI demos and I do not know which ones are real."
- "The board wants an AI strategy and I do not have one."

**Buying triggers:**

- Lost a key employee (urgency spike)
- New funding round or revenue milestone (budget unlocked)
- Failed agency engagement (trust gap — we fill it)
- Board asks "What is your AI strategy?" (political pressure)
- Quarterly planning season (budget allocation window)
- Competitive pressure — a competitor shipped something AI-powered

**How they decide:**

In conversations, not committees. They want to feel the person across from them gets their business. The price needs to fit without a calculator. And they need to believe they can have a working specialist live within a week. Three things: **trust the person, trust the price, trust the speed.**

**How to sell to SMB:**

Lead with the Free Workflow Assessment. Move to Starter Team ($2,800/mo for 3 specialists) or a department bundle. The close is the pilot: "Let us put one specialist on your biggest bottleneck. Three days. If it does not work, you walk away." SMB deals should close in 7-14 days. If you are past 14 days, something is stuck — find it and address it.

---

## 2.4 Enterprise Persona — "The AI-Curious Executive"

**Who they are:**

- CTO, VP Operations, VP Innovation, Chief AI Officer, Head of Transformation
- Company revenue $10M+, often $50M-$500M
- Has a budget line item for "AI initiatives" or "workforce transformation"
- Has likely run one or two failed AI pilots already (they know what bad looks like)
- Surrounded by vendors promising AI transformation; deeply skeptical
- Needs to show measurable results to a board or executive team

**What keeps them up at night:**

- "I have to show measurable AI ROI to the board this year."
- "My last AI pilot was a science project that never shipped."
- "Procurement and security review will kill anything that is not enterprise-grade."
- "I need something my team can adopt without a 6-month training program."
- "Every vendor says they are different. None of them are."

**How they decide:**

Through process. Evaluation, pilot, security review, procurement, legal. The cycle is longer — 30-90 days — but the contracts are bigger and stickier. The key: find the **operator** inside the enterprise. The person who actually does the work and feels the pain. Sell to them. Let them sell internally.

**How to sell to enterprise:**

**30-day, $10,000 paid pilot.** Not a free trial. Free trials attract tire-kickers. Paid pilots attract decision-makers. Lead with four pilot deliverables:

1. **Workflow Constraint Map** — Where their current process breaks down
2. **Risk & Control Design** — How Cavi handles their specific security and compliance requirements
3. **Implementation Blueprint** — Phased rollout plan with timelines and success metrics
4. **Executive Decision Ready Readout** — Board-ready summary of pilot findings, ROI projection, and go/no-go recommendation

> "The pilot is not a demo with a timer. It is a 30-day engagement with four deliverables that your team can take to the board. At the end, you have the evidence to say yes or no. Either way, you walk away with the Workflow Constraint Map and the Implementation Blueprint."

# Part 3. The Sales Motion

---

## 3.1 The Velocity Framework

Cavi sells on velocity. Not pressure — velocity. The goal is to compress the time between "first conversation" and "working specialist deployed" to the absolute minimum, because the product sells itself once it is running.

**The 7-14 Day Deal Cycle:**

| Day | Milestone | Owner |
|-----|-----------|-------|
| **Day 0** | First conversation (inbound or outbound) | Account Executive or Founders |
| **Day 1-3** | Free Workflow Assessment delivered | Account Executive (Tier A/B) or Founders (Tier C) |
| **Day 4-7** | Decision conversation — pricing presented, objections handled | Account Executive + Founders (if enterprise) |
| **Day 7-14** | Contract signed and first specialist live | Account Executive owns close, delivery begins |

**The 14-Day Rule:** If a deal is not closing within 14 days of the first conversation, something is blocking it. Do not wait. Ask directly:

> "We are at Day [X]. What is the one thing that needs to happen for you to say yes or no? I would rather hear no today than maybe next month."

This is not aggressive — it is respectful of their time and yours. Operators appreciate directness.

---

## 3.2 Who Sells What — Role Definitions

| Role | Owns | Does Not Own |
|------|------|-------------|
| **Founders** | Enterprise pilots, strategic accounts, escalations, founding customer relationships. Support Account Executives on calls when needed. Set pricing policy and discount authority. | Solo and Duo cycles after the first 10 deals close. Day-to-day SMB pipeline management. |
| **Account Executives** | SMB primary motion, Starter Team and Department Bundle deals, Solo/Duo at scale. Own the pipeline, own the follow-up, own the close. | Enterprise security reviews, MSA negotiation, discount authority above 25%. |
| **Partners / Referral Channel** | Solopreneur and Solo tier deals, vertical-specific introductions, warm referrals. | Direct enterprise pursuit. Price negotiation beyond standard tiers. |

**Escalation rules:**

- If an Account Executive needs Founders on a call: 24-hour notice, brief in writing (who, what they need, what the block is).
- If a partner surfaces an enterprise lead: hand off to Founders within 48 hours with full context.
- If anyone encounters a security or compliance question they cannot answer: escalate to the engineering lead. Do not guess.

## 3.3 Free Workflow Assessment — Ownership Tiers

The Free Workflow Assessment is the top-of-funnel offer for every segment. It is free, it is valuable, and it is the fastest way to build trust and qualify a prospect simultaneously.

**What it is:** A 30-minute conversation (video or phone) that produces a **Mini Workflow Map** — a one-page visual showing the prospect's current workflow, where the bottlenecks are, and where Cavi specialists would plug in.

**What it is not:** A sales pitch disguised as a consultation. Do real work. Identify real bottlenecks. If Cavi is not the right fit, say so. The assessment builds trust whether or not it leads to a deal.

| Tier | Segment | Owner | Duration | Turnaround |
|------|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| **Tier A** | Solo and Solopreneur deals | Account Executives or Partners | 30 minutes | Immediate — Mini Workflow Map delivered same day |
| **Tier B** | SMB deals | Account Executives (Founders available for strategic SMB accounts) | 30-45 minutes | 48-hour turnaround on Mini Workflow Map |
| **Tier C** | Enterprise deals | Founders join the Account Executive on the call | 45-60 minutes | Evolves into paid 30-day Enterprise Pilot |

**After the assessment:**

- **Tier A:** "Based on what we mapped, here is the one specialist that would make the biggest difference. Want to try it for 14 days, no card required?"
- **Tier B:** "I mapped three bottlenecks. Here is how a Starter Team of three specialists would address all of them. Can we schedule a decision conversation for Thursday?"
- **Tier C:** "This is more complex than a single assessment can cover. The right next step is our 30-day Enterprise Pilot — $10,000, four deliverables, and your team walks away with a complete implementation blueprint whether you continue with Cavi or not."

---

## 3.4 Sales-to-Delivery Handoff

**Solo / Duo / Starter Team:** ONE PERSON owns end to end. The person who closes the deal is the person who onboards the client. No handoff. No "your account manager will reach out." The client sees one face from first conversation to first output. This is a feature, not a limitation.

**Department Bundle and Enterprise:** Hand off at contract signing. The Account Executive (with Founders supporting on enterprise deals) closes and writes a brief — one page, covering: client name, key contact, what they bought, what the first deliverable is, timeline, and any promises made during the sale. The delivery team picks up from there.

**The handoff brief must include:**

1. Client name and key contact (name, email, phone, preferred communication channel)
2. What they bought (tier, number of specialists, divisions activated)
3. What the first deliverable is (specific, not vague — "weekly content calendar for LinkedIn" not "marketing help")
4. Timeline commitments made during the sale
5. Any special terms, discounts, or promises
6. Known concerns or objections that were addressed (so delivery does not re-open them)

---

## 3.5 Flexible Fit

Cavi scales from a 2-agent bundle to a full multi-division deployment. If a prospect has workflows, we can add leverage. The platform adapts to the business — not the other way around.

**No business is too small.** A solopreneur with one specialist is a real client with real needs. Treat them accordingly.

**No business is too complex.** For compliance requirements still in progress, the pilot program is designed to build confidence and surface any gaps early. For unusual workflows, the Free Workflow Assessment maps the path before any commitment.

**The principle:** We do not turn prospects away because they do not fit a predefined box. We map their workflows, identify where leverage is possible, and propose the right configuration. If a prospect needs something we do not have yet, that is a roadmap conversation — not a disqualification.

> "Cavi is not a one-size-fits-all product. It is a workforce that configures to your business. Tell us what you need done, and we will show you how the specialists handle it — or tell you honestly if we are not there yet."

# Part 4. Discovery & Qualification

---

## 4.1 The Velocity Qualification Framework

Five questions. Every discovery call. Every segment. If you cannot answer all five by the end of the call, you do not have a qualified lead — you have a conversation.

| # | Category | The Question | What You Are Really Asking |
|---|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | **PAIN** | "What is the work that is not getting done right now?" | Is there a real gap, or are they browsing? |
| 2 | **URGENCY** | "What changes if you do not solve this in 30 days?" | Is there a deadline, a consequence, a trigger? |
| 3 | **AUTHORITY** | "Who else needs to nod for this to move?" | Are you talking to the decision maker, or do you need to get to them? |
| 4 | **BUDGET FIT** | "Does $1,200 to $7,500 a month fit your operating budget?" | Can they actually pay for this? |
| 5 | **FIRST DELIVERABLE** | "If we made one thing live in 3 days, what would it be?" | Can they articulate what they need? If yes, they are qualified. |

**How to use the framework:**

- **All five answered = qualified.** Move to pricing and close.
- **Four of five answered = almost qualified.** Identify the missing one and address it directly.
- **Three or fewer = not ready.** Offer the Free Workflow Assessment and re-engage when they have clarity.

---

## 4.2 Discovery Question Bank

### Opening Questions (First 5 minutes)

> "Tell me about your business and what your team looks like today."

> "What made you take this call? What prompted you to look at something like Cavi right now?"

> "How are you handling [the specific workflow they mentioned] today?"

> "Walk me through a typical week. Where do you spend time that you wish you did not?"

> "What have you tried before to solve this? What worked and what did not?"

### Middle Questions (Minutes 5-20)

> "If I could wave a wand and fix one thing in your operations tomorrow, what would it be?"

> "How many people touch that workflow today? What does it cost you — in time, money, or missed opportunities?"

> "What does your tech stack look like? What tools are you already using?"

> "Who on your team would interact with a Cavi specialist day to day?"

> "Have you used AI tools before? ChatGPT, Claude, anything else? What was that experience like?"

> "What does success look like in 90 days? If Cavi works perfectly, what is different about your business?"

> "Is there a specific event, deadline, or milestone driving the timeline here?"

### Closing Questions (Last 5-10 minutes)

> "Based on what you have told me, here is what I would recommend. Does that feel right to you?"

> "What would need to be true for you to say yes this week?"

> "Is there anything that would make you say no right now? I would rather hear it."

> "Who else on your team should see this before you decide?"

> "Can we schedule the next conversation for [specific day and time]?"

## 4.3 Velocity Signals — Read the Room

| Signal | What It Means | What to Do |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| They ask about pricing early | They are ready to buy. Do not delay. | Give the price. Do not make them wait for a "pricing call." |
| They ask about implementation timeline | They are visualizing using it. | Reinforce "live in 3 days" and offer to start immediately. |
| They mention a specific workflow or bottleneck | They have a real pain point, not abstract interest. | Anchor everything to that specific workflow for the rest of the call. |
| They say "I need to think about it" | There is an unspoken objection. | Ask: "What specifically do you need to think through? I might be able to answer it right now." |

| They compare you to a specific tool or vendor | They are evaluating. They need differentiation. | Use the three-way frame from Part 7. Never name the competitor. |
| They start talking about their team's reaction | They are already thinking about adoption. | Pivot to the "superpowers" frame: "Your team gets amplified, not replaced." |
| They go silent after pricing | Sticker shock or internal calculation. | Wait. Let them process. Then ask: "What are you comparing that number to?" |
| They ask for a free trial | They want proof before commitment. | Solo/Duo/Starter: "14 days free, no card required." Larger: "Free Assessment first, then $1,500 pilot." |
| They forward your email to someone else | They are building internal consensus. | Ask who they forwarded to and offer to talk to that person directly. |

## 4.4 Fit Assessment

Every prospect gets a fair assessment. The goal is not to disqualify — it is to find the right fit and set honest expectations.

**For compliance requirements (SOC 2, HIPAA, FedRAMP):**

> "We're building toward enterprise compliance. The pilot program is designed to surface requirements early and build confidence. Let us show you how we handle security today — encryption at rest and in transit, role-based access, full audit trails, and data isolation. For specific certifications on your roadmap, our engineering lead can walk through our compliance timeline."

**For on-premise requirements:**

> "We offer hybrid deployment options — Kubernetes on your infrastructure or Cavi-hosted managed service. Let's map the right topology during scoping. The Enterprise Pilot is designed to evaluate exactly this: what runs where, what data stays where, and how the architecture fits your requirements."

**For complex procurement processes:**

> "The 30-day Enterprise Pilot is built specifically to give your procurement and security teams the evidence they need — four deliverables, fixed scope, clear outcomes. We have been through evaluation processes before and we know what your team needs to see."

**For RFP processes:**

> "We participate in structured evaluations. The pilot deliverables — Workflow Constraint Map, Risk & Control Design, Implementation Blueprint, and Executive Readout — are built for exactly this purpose. They give your evaluation team concrete evidence, not marketing materials."

**For prospects with very early-stage needs:**

> "It sounds like you are still figuring out where AI fits in your business. That is exactly what the Free Workflow Assessment is for. Thirty minutes, no commitment, and you walk away with a workflow map whether you buy Cavi or not. Want to schedule that?"

## 11.2 Foundation Model Glossary

Know these terms. You do not need to explain them in detail — you need to use them correctly.

| Term | Definition | When You Use It |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Foundation Model** | A large AI model (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, etc.) trained on broad data. The raw intelligence layer. | "We are model-agnostic — we route between multiple foundation models." |
| **Fine-Tuning** | Training a foundation model on specific data to improve it for a narrow task. | "Our specialists use fine-tuned models where it improves output quality for specific workflows." |
| **Prompt Engineering** | Crafting the instructions given to a model to get better output. | "Each specialist has purpose-built prompts engineered for their specific task." |
| **RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** | Giving a model access to external data (documents, databases) so it can reference real information instead of relying only on training data. | "Our specialists use RAG to work with your business documents — they reference your actual data, not just their training." |
| **Embedding** | Converting text into a numerical representation that captures meaning. Used for search, similarity, and retrieval. | "We use embeddings to help specialists find relevant information in your documents." |
| **Token** | The basic unit of text that models process. Roughly 0.75 words per token. | "Tokens are how model usage is measured. Our Model Budget covers typical volume." |
| **Context Window** | The amount of text a model can consider at once. Measured in tokens. | "Modern models have large context windows — they can work with long documents in a single pass." |
| **Inference** | The process of a model generating output from input. Each API call is an inference. | "Inference costs are what the Model Budget covers." |
| **Latency** | The time between sending a request to a model and getting a response. | "We optimize for low latency — specialists respond quickly, not in minutes." |

## 11.3 Architecture Glossary

| Term | Definition | When You Use It |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Microservices** | An architecture where each component runs independently and communicates through APIs. Each specialist is a microservice. | "Each specialist runs as an independent microservice — isolated, scalable, and independently deployable." |
| **Kubernetes (K8s)** | An orchestration platform that manages containerized applications at scale. | "We run on Kubernetes — industry-standard orchestration for containerized workloads." |
| **Container** | A lightweight, isolated environment that packages an application and its dependencies. | "Each specialist runs in its own container — fully isolated from other specialists and other clients." |
| **Multi-Cloud** | Running infrastructure across multiple cloud providers (AWS, GCP, Azure) for redundancy and flexibility. | "We are multi-cloud — not locked into any single provider." |
| **API Gateway** | A single entry point that routes requests to the appropriate backend service. | "All requests flow through our API gateway — centralized routing, authentication, and rate limiting." |
| **Message Queue** | A system for asynchronous communication between services. | "Specialists communicate through message queues — reliable, ordered, and auditable." |
| **Load Balancer** | Distributes incoming requests across multiple instances of a service. | "Load balancing ensures consistent performance even under heavy usage." |
| **Data Isolation** | Ensuring one client's data is completely separate from another's. | "Full data isolation — your data never comingles with another client's data." |
| **Division** | A self-contained department within the Cavi platform, led by an orchestrator and staffed with domain specialists. Each division has its own workspace, capability set, and integration stack. | "Cavi is organized into 8 divisions, each led by an orchestrator who coordinates the specialists within it." |
| **Orchestrator** | The lead agent in a division. It delegates work to specialists, coordinates handoffs, and reports back to the operator for approval. Orchestrators do not execute tasks directly — they coordinate. | "Each division has an orchestrator that manages the specialists, coordinates work, and ensures nothing falls through the cracks." |

## 11.4 AI Engineering Glossary

| Term | Definition | When You Use It |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **Agent** | An AI system that can take actions, use tools, and work toward goals — not just generate text. | "Our specialists are agents — they take actions, use tools, and produce deliverables." |
| **Tool Use** | An agent's ability to call external tools (APIs, databases, file systems) as part of its work. | "Specialists use tools — they can search databases, call APIs, generate files, and interact with your tech stack." |
| **Chain of Thought** | A technique where the model reasons step-by-step before producing a final answer. Improves accuracy. | "Our specialists use chain-of-thought reasoning for complex tasks — step-by-step processing, not one-shot guessing." |
| **Model Routing** | Selecting the optimal model for each specific task based on requirements (speed, accuracy, cost, capability). | "We route between multiple models — each task goes to the model that handles it best." |
| **Fallback** | An alternative model or process that activates if the primary one fails or underperforms. | "Every specialist has fallback models — if the primary model is unavailable, the specialist switches automatically." |
| **Guardrails** | Rules and constraints that prevent a model from producing harmful, off-topic, or incorrect output. | "Every specialist has guardrails — scope limits, content filters, and output validation." |
| **Evaluation (Evals)** | Systematic testing of model output quality against defined criteria. | "We run continuous evaluations to ensure specialist output meets quality standards." |

## 11.5 Security and Compliance Glossary

| Term | Definition | When You Use It |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **SOC 2 Type II** | An audit standard that verifies a company's security controls over time. The gold standard for SaaS security. | "We are building toward SOC 2 Type II. Here is what we do today..." (never claim we have it) |
| **RBAC (Role-Based Access Control)** | Limiting access based on a user's role — admins see everything, operators see their division, viewers see output only. | "Full RBAC — your team members see only what their role allows." |
| **SSO (Single Sign-On)** | Allowing users to log in with their existing company credentials (Google Workspace, Okta, Azure AD). | "SSO integration — your team logs in with their existing identity provider." |
| **Encryption at Rest** | Data is encrypted when stored on disk. | "All data encrypted at rest using industry-standard encryption." |
| **Encryption in Transit** | Data is encrypted when moving between systems (TLS/HTTPS). | "All data encrypted in transit — TLS everywhere." |
| **Audit Trail** | A complete, tamper-proof log of every action, input, output, and decision. | "Full audit trail — every action logged and reviewable." |
| **Data Residency** | Where data is physically stored (which country, which region). | "We can discuss data residency requirements during scoping." |
| **Penetration Testing** | Security testing where experts attempt to break into the system to find vulnerabilities. | "Penetration testing is part of our security program." |
| **Zero Trust** | A security model that verifies every request, even from inside the network. | "Zero trust architecture — every request is authenticated and authorized." |

> This means the specialist references your actual data — your style guides, your product specs, your customer data — not just its training. The documents stay in your isolated environment and are never used to train models.

## 11.7 When to Bring In Technical

**Escalation triggers — bring in the engineering lead when:**

- The prospect asks about specific encryption algorithms, key management, or cryptographic standards
- The prospect asks about specific compliance certifications (SOC 2 audit details, HIPAA BAA terms, FedRAMP authorization)
- The prospect asks about penetration testing results, vulnerability reports, or security incident response procedures
- The prospect asks about specific model versions, model fine-tuning details, or prompt engineering approaches
- The prospect asks about data residency in specific jurisdictions with legal requirements
- The prospect's security team wants a technical deep-dive or architecture review
- You are not confident in your answer — escalate rather than guess

**Escalation script:**

> "That is a great question and the right person to walk you through it is our engineering lead. They can go as deep as your team wants on the architecture, the security controls, and the implementation details. Can I set up a 30-minute technical session with them this week?"

**How to escalate well:**

1. Acknowledge the question: "Great question."
2. Give the category-level answer if you can: "At a high level, we do X."
3. Escalate for depth: "For the implementation specifics, our engineering lead is the right person."
4. Offer a specific next step: "Can I schedule a 30-minute technical session?"
5. Brief the engineering lead before the call with the specific questions.

## 11.8 What to Never Say in Technical Conversations

| Never Say This | Why | Say This Instead |
|---------------|-----|-----------------|
| Specific model names and versions to clients | Models change; commitments to specific models create problems | "We route between multiple foundation models, selecting the best for each task." |
| "Our models do not hallucinate" | All models can hallucinate; lying about it destroys credibility | "We have four layers of defense against hallucination: narrow scope, human approval, audit trails, and model routing." |
| Specific cloud provider names in client-facing materials | Limits flexibility and creates vendor-lock-in perception | "Multi-cloud infrastructure with redundancy." |
| Any compliance certification we do not have | Legal and regulatory exposure | "We are building toward [certification]. Here is what we do today." |
| Specific performance benchmarks we have not verified | Creates commitments we may not meet | "We project [X] based on internal usage. The pilot will show your specific numbers." |
| Internal tool names or proprietary system names | Proprietary information | Use category language: "our orchestration layer," "our routing system." |
| "We are more secure than [competitor]" | Unverifiable and unprofessional | "Here is what our security architecture looks like." |
| "We will have [feature] by [date]" unless confirmed by the engineering lead | Roadmap commitments in sales conversations create delivery pressure and broken promises | "That is on our roadmap. I can get you a timeline from the development team." |
